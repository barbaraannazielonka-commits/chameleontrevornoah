Section 01
Who is Trevor Noah?
Teacher note: Use this as a 10-minute warm-up. Ask students: what do they know about South Africa? Have they heard of Trevor Noah?
Born a Crime

Trevor Noah was born on 20 February 1984 in Johannesburg, South Africa. His mother, Patricia, was Black Xhosa, and his father, Robert, was White Swiss-German. Under apartheid law, their relationship was illegal — and so was Trevor's very existence. The title of his autobiography, Born a Crime, refers to this directly.

🎤 Comedian & Host

Trevor Noah hosted The Daily Show on Comedy Central (USA) from 2015 to 2022. The show uses humour and satire to comment on politics and current events. He became one of the most famous South African voices in the world.

📖 Born a Crime (2016)

His autobiography tells the story of growing up in South Africa during and after apartheid. Chameleon, the text you will read today, is extracted from this book. It explores language, identity, and survival.

Did you know? Trevor Noah grew up speaking six languages: Xhosa, Zulu, Sotho, Tswana, Afrikaans, and English. As you will read, he used language as a tool to survive and to belong.
Section 02
Apartheid in South Africa
Teacher note: Allow ~15 minutes for this section. The background box from the text is reproduced here. Ask students to read the key facts before moving on.
From the text:
Apartheid started in 1948, giving Whites total control over South Africa. Blacks, who at that time made up 87% of the population, lived on only 13% of the land. During apartheid, they could not vote, own land, be in relationships with Whites or even live in the same areas. Rebellion within the country, led by Nelson Mandela, and pressure from countries all over the world led to the end of apartheid in 1994.
1948: The National Party wins the election in South Africa and officially introduces apartheid — a system of racial segregation meaning "separateness" in Afrikaans.
The Population Registration Act: Every South African was classified by race: White, Coloured, Indian, or Black (Bantu). This classification determined almost every aspect of a person's life.
Pass Laws: Black South Africans had to carry a passbook at all times to travel in "white areas." Failure to show the passbook meant arrest.
Bantustans: Black South Africans were forced to live in "homelands" (bantustans) — 13% of the land for 87% of the population.
The Immorality Act: Sexual relations and marriage between Whites and people of other races were illegal. This is why Trevor Noah was literally "born a crime."
1960 – Sharpeville Massacre: Police open fire on peaceful protesters, killing 69 people. The ANC (African National Congress), led by Nelson Mandela, is banned.
1964 – Mandela imprisoned: Nelson Mandela is sentenced to life in prison on Robben Island.
1990: F.W. de Klerk unbans the ANC and releases Mandela after 27 years in prison.
1994: South Africa holds its first fully democratic election. Nelson Mandela becomes president. Apartheid officially ends.
Apartheid
Afrikaans for "separateness." A system of institutionalised racial segregation in South Africa (1948–1994).
Segregation
Enforced separation of racial groups in public spaces, schools, transport, etc.
ANC
African National Congress. The political party that led the resistance against apartheid.
Township
Urban areas designated for non-white residents, often poorly serviced. Soweto is a famous example.
Afrikaans
A language developed from Dutch, spoken by the White Afrikaner community that controlled the government during apartheid.
Xhosa / Zulu
Two of South Africa's major indigenous languages and ethnic groups. Trevor Noah's mother was Xhosa.
Section 03
Chameleon — Read the Text
Teacher note: Give students 15–20 minutes to read carefully. Key passages are highlighted. Encourage annotation: what surprises them? What do they want to ask?
📖 Read carefully — ~15 min

As a kid I understood that people were different colors, but in my head white and black and brown were like types of chocolate. Dad was the white chocolate, mom was the dark chocolate, and I was the milk chocolate. But we were all just chocolate. I didn't know any of it had anything to do with "race." I didn't know what race was. My mother never referred to my dad as white or to me as mixed. So when the other kids in Soweto called me "white," even though I was light brown, I just thought they had their colors mixed up, like they hadn't learned them properly. "Ah, yes, my friend. You've confused aqua with turquoise. I can see how you made that mistake. You're not the first."

I soon learned that the quickest way to bridge the race gap was through language. Soweto was a melting pot: families from different tribes and homelands. Most kids in the township spoke only their home language, but I learned several languages because I grew up in a house where there was no option but to learn them. My mom made sure English was the first language I spoke. If you're black in South Africa, speaking English is the one thing that can give you a leg up. English is the language of money. English comprehension is equated with intelligence. If you're looking for a job, English is the difference between getting the job or staying unemployed. If you're standing in the dock, English is the difference between getting off with a fine or going to prison.

After English, Xhosa was what we spoke around the house. When my mother was angry she'd fall back on her home language. As a naughty child, I was well versed in Xhosa threats. They were the first phrases I picked up, mostly for my own safety— phrases like "Ndiza kubetha entloko." "I'll knock you upside the head." Or "Sidenge ndini somntwana." "You idiot of a child." It's a very passionate language. Outside of that, my mother picked up different languages here and there. She learned Zulu because it's similar to Xhosa. She spoke German because of my father. She spoke Afrikaans because it is useful to know the language of your oppressor. Sotho she learned in the streets.

Living with my mom, I saw how she used language to cross boundaries, handle situations, navigate the world. We were in a shop once, and the shopkeeper, right in front of us, turned to his security guard and said, in Afrikaans, "Volg daai swartes, netnou steel hulle iets." "Follow those blacks in case they steal something."

My mother turned around and said, in beautiful, fluent Afrikaans, "Hoekom volg jy nie daai swartes sodat jy hulle kan help kry waarna hulle soek nie?" "Why don't you follow these blacks so you can help them find what they're looking for?"

"Ag, jammer!" he said, apologizing in Afrikaans. Then—and this was the funny thing—he didn't apologize for being racist; he merely apologized for aiming his racism at us. "Oh, I'm so sorry," he said. "I thought you were like the other blacks. You know how they love to steal."

I learned to use language like my mother did. I would simulcast—give you the program in your own tongue. I'd get suspicious looks from people just walking down the street. "Where are you from?" they'd ask. I'd reply in whatever language they'd addressed me in, using the same accent that they used. There would be a brief moment of confusion, and then the suspicious look would disappear. "Oh, okay. I thought you were a stranger. We're good then."

It became a tool that served me my whole life. One day as a young man I was walking down the street, and a group of Zulu guys was walking behind me, closing in on me, and I could hear them talking to one another about how they were going to mug me. "Asibambe le autie yomlungu. Phuma ngapha mina ngizoqhamuka ngemuva kwakhe." "Let's get this white guy. You go to his left, and I'll come up behind him." I didn't know what to do. I couldn't run, so I just spun around real quick and said, "Kodwa bafwethu yingani singavele sibambe umuntu inkunzi? Asenzeni. Mina ngikulindele." "Yo, guys, why don't we just mug someone together? I'm ready. Let's do it."

They looked shocked for a moment, and then they started laughing. "Oh, sorry, dude. We thought you were something else. We weren't trying to take anything from you. We were trying to steal from white people. Have a good day, man." They were ready to do me violent harm, until they felt we were part of the same tribe, and then we were cool. That, and so many other smaller incidents in my life, made me realize that language, even more than color, defines who you are to people.

I became a chameleon. My color didn't change, but I could change your perception of my color. If you spoke to me in Zulu, I replied to you in Zulu. If you spoke to me in Tswana, I replied to you in Tswana. Maybe I didn't look like you, but if I spoke like you, I was you.

(Extract from Born a Crime)

Glossary: melting pot = a place where people of different backgrounds mix · simulcast = broadcast in multiple formats at once · to mug = to rob someone in public · to bridge the gap = to reduce a difference · dock = the place in a courtroom where the accused stands · oppressor = someone who uses power to keep others down

Section 05
Text Tasks
Teacher note: Tasks 1–3 are reading comprehension tasks (individual, ~20 min). Tasks 4–5 are analysis and discussion (pairs/groups). These are designed for VG1 academic level.
⏱ ~5 min · Individual
Reading Comprehension
1 — What does Trevor say about language?

Read the text carefully and answer these questions in your own words:

  1. According to Trevor, what is the quickest way to bridge the race gap?
  2. What does Trevor say English gives you in South Africa? Give three specific examples from the text.
  3. Why did Trevor's mother learn Afrikaans? What does this tell us about power and language?
  4. What happened in the shop? Why is this scene important?
⏱ ~5 min · Individual
Reading Comprehension
2 — The Mugging Scene

Re-read the scene where Trevor is almost mugged by a group of Zulu men (paragraph beginning "One day as a young man…").

  1. Why were the Zulu men originally going to mug Trevor?
  2. What did Trevor do to avoid being mugged?
  3. How did the men react, and why?
  4. Trevor says: "language, even more than color, defines who you are to people." Do you agree? Use the mugging scene to support your answer.
⏱ ~5 min · Individual
Reading Comprehension
3 — The Chocolate Metaphor

Trevor opens the text by comparing people to types of chocolate.

  1. What does the chocolate metaphor tell us about how Trevor understood race as a child?
  2. How is this childhood view different from how South African society viewed race at the time?
  3. Do you think this is an effective metaphor? Why / why not?
⏱ ~8 min · Pairs
Discussion
4 — The Chameleon Metaphor

Trevor calls himself a "chameleon." Discuss with a partner:

  • In what ways is Trevor like a chameleon? List at least three specific examples from the text.
  • Is being a chameleon a strength, a survival strategy, or both? Is there anything lost when you constantly adapt?
  • Can you think of situations in your own life where you "adapt" your language or behaviour depending on who you are with? (Think: speaking to teachers vs. friends, speaking Norwegian vs. English, etc.)

The academic term for changing language depending on context is code-switching. Have you heard of this term?

⏱ ~8 min · Groups of 3–4
Class Discussion
5 — Apartheid & Today: Does It Still Matter?

Discuss in groups, then share with the class:

  • Apartheid ended in 1994. Do you think the effects of apartheid are still felt in South Africa today? Why / why not?
  • Trevor was born in 1984 — ten years before apartheid ended. He grew up in a system that said he should not exist. How might that shape a person's identity and sense of humour?
  • Are there systems in the world today that are similar to apartheid? (Think: discrimination based on race, language, religion, class.)

Bonus · Reflection
B3 — "Born a Crime": What's in a Title?

The title of Trevor Noah's autobiography is Born a Crime.

  1. What does this title mean literally? (Why was he "born a crime"?)
  2. What could the title mean metaphorically or symbolically?
  3. Do you think it is an effective title? Why? Can you think of an alternative title for this extract?
Bonus · Deeper Reading
B4 — Autobiography as a Genre

Born a Crime is an autobiography — a person's own account of their life.

  1. What are the advantages of telling this story through an autobiography? What can an autobiography do that a history book cannot?
  2. Find three stylistic features in the text that make it feel personal and authentic. Think about: voice, humour, dialogue, examples.
  3. Trevor mixes serious topics with humour throughout the text. Find one example of this combination and discuss why he might have chosen to write this way.
